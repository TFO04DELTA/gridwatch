What this is

GRIDWATCH is a public-interest instrument that asks two questions with public data: are regional electricity monopolies becoming less reliable, and is data center construction correlated with that decline and with rising costs to ordinary ratepayers.

It polls the live outage maps of 30+ investor-owned utilities from Ohio to Georgia every few minutes, attributes each outage to weather or not, measures proximity to data centers in active construction, and correlates all of it against the utilities' own reliability metrics, the wholesale capacity prices customers pay, and the interconnection pipeline of what is coming next.

It is built to be argued with. Every classification rule, control, and data source is described below, and the instrument is designed to refuse overclaiming — it shows confidence intervals and says plainly when the data does not yet support a conclusion.

How outages are classified

Every outage the utilities report is placed into one of four buckets, shown as colored dots on the map:

tagmeaning
weather-likely The outage coincides with adverse weather at its location — recent wind, precipitation, or an active National Weather Service warning/watch. These are expected; storms cause outages.
fair-weather No adverse weather nearby. An outage in calm conditions points to equipment, capacity, or grid-stress causes rather than nature. This is the signal the project cares about.
near active DC site A fair-weather outage within the ring of a data center in active change — under construction, contested, or newly announced. These are counted as evidence.
near operating DC (context) Near a data center that is already running. Shown for context but never counted as evidence — an operating facility is not construction-phase grid stress.

Weather attribution uses hourly conditions from Open-Meteo (trailing three hours) plus active NWS alerts, both keyless public sources. A "fair-weather" label means the classifier found no adverse weather — it is a statement about the weather, not a claim about the true cause.

Why the controls matter

The honest test. If outages cluster near data-center construction, the skeptical explanation is: "that is just where everything is being built — of course the grid is disturbed." To separate data centers from construction in general, GRIDWATCH tracks control corridors: fast-growing areas with heavy construction and no data center.

If active-DC sites and control corridors show the same fair-weather outage rate, the honest conclusion is "growth corridor," not "data center," and the instrument says so. Only a rate that is higher near data centers than near matched controls supports the thesis.

The Evidence tab compares the two using 95% Wilson confidence intervals. When the intervals overlap, it displays "not yet statistically distinguishable — keep collecting." It only reports a difference when the intervals separate. A single day is noise; the comparison needs weeks of accumulation, and a null result is published exactly as prominently as a positive one.

The monopoly-reliability scorecard

Most Americans cannot choose their electricity distributor — a single regulated utility owns the wire in each territory. GRIDWATCH pairs each feed with that utility's own SAIDI and SAIFI figures reported annually to the U.S. Energy Information Administration:

  • SAIDI — System Average Interruption Duration Index: the average minutes per year a customer spends without power. Higher is worse.
  • SAIFI — System Average Interruption Frequency Index: the average number of interruptions per customer per year.

These are the industry's own numbers, filed with a federal agency. Ranking captive-customer territories by their reported unreliability is not our framing imposed on the data — it is the monopolies' self-report, sorted worst-first.

Brownouts and grid strain

True brownout data — deliberate voltage reductions — is measured at the feeder level and kept internal by utilities; no one publishes it. GRIDWATCH uses two honest proxies:

  • Grid strain: actual electricity demand versus the day-ahead forecast (EIA-930), per grid operator. Sustained demand at or above forecast is the condition under which conservation appeals and voltage reductions occur. Preliminary (unsettled) recent hours are excluded so the figure is not inflated.
  • Momentary events: outages seen in only a single poll — the standard engineering proxy (MAIFI-style) for brief protection events that are brownout-adjacent.

The cost transfer

Data centers do not only consume power — their grid connections are frequently paid for through rates charged to all customers. GRIDWATCH assembles the receipts: PJM capacity-auction clearing prices (what customers pay for guaranteed supply), demand growth in data-center-heavy zones, the interconnection queue of requests near tracked sites (what is coming), and retired power plants beside those sites (campuses often inherit a dead plant's connection rights). Where a specific cost-shift is documented in a regulatory docket, it is listed on the Watch List with its source.

On PJM prices: PJM's Data Miner 2 terms prohibit redistribution of derived data without a license, so zone-level LMP data is used only privately. The public site uses EIA-930 (public domain) for the demand signal instead.

What GRIDWATCH does not claim

  • It does not claim any specific outage was caused by a data center. It measures proximity and weather, and aggregates. Causation is what the controls and confidence intervals are there to test, over time.
  • "Fair-weather" means no adverse weather was found — not that the cause is known.
  • Data center locations flagged verify are best-effort estimates pending parcel confirmation; sites marked context-only are plotted but excluded from all evidence math.
  • Florida and other hurricane-dominated territories are deliberately excluded — storm noise would swamp the fair-weather signal. Scope honesty is worth more than coverage.
  • The instrument is one person's public-interest project, not an official or utility source. Every number links back to its origin so anyone can check it.

Reproduce it

All code and data are open. The poller, configuration, and every emitted dataset live in the repository; the raw snapshots are downloadable as JSON and GeoJSON, with an RSS digest. See the source on GitHub.

GRIDWATCH is a public-interest research instrument. It uses public data, shows its methods, and publishes null results. Corrections and scrutiny are welcome via the repository.
